Training Data Bias

ND045 DSBL L04 Training Data Bias

Data Bias Summary

In building a machine learning model, we distinguish between the data that we will use to train that model, which we call the "training data," with a subset of that data that is held out from model training but used to test the fit of the model, which we call the "test data."

For your model to perform well in production, it is critically important that the training data used to build that model be as representative as possible of the data that will ultimately pass to that model in production. For example, consider the case of a natural language processing-based message classifier. As customer inquiry messages come in, they may specifically address issues with specific product lines. If these messages can be automatically classified and routed to specialists particular to those lines, we can save a lot of time and provide better customer service. We run into a challenge when product lines change. If we train our model based on data from the first half of the year, and then in the second half of the year we launch a new product line, we shouldn't expect our model to be able to classify messages according to that new product line because it was never trained on messages associated with that product line. In this case, the training data is not representative of the data that is encountered by the model in production.

There are two particular forms of bias that may occur in training data:

  1. Convenience bias. Some types of data may be easier to collect than other types of data. If this more convenient data is not fully representative of the full variety of data that will be encountered in production, this can skew the data you have available in your ground truth data set, the one you use to train your model, which then skews your machine learning model.
  2. Recency bias. You may have gathered more data recently than in the past for some reason. This may be fine, unless that data is skewed for some reason, and does not represent the full variety of data that the machine learning model will encounter in the future. It is critically important to ensure that any machine learning model has been built on data that is most representative of how that model will be used in the future.

Key Terms:

  • Training Data: Subset of your data used to train the machine learning model.
  • Testing Data: Subset of your data held out from model training used to test model fit.
  • Convenience bias: Some types of data may be easier to collect than other types of data, thus skewing the data you have available in your ground truth dataset, and then skewing your ML model.
  • Recency bias: You may have gathered more data recently than in the past for some reason, and that recent data may be skewed for some reason, and not sufficiently represent the full variety of data that your ML model will encounter in the future.
