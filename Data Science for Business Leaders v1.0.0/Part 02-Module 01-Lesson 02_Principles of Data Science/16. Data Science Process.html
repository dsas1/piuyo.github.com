16. Data Science Process

Data Science Process Heading

Data Science Process

ND045 DSBL L01 Data Science Process

Steps of the Data Science Process

  1. Identify Opportunity and KPI’s, determine success criteria
  2. Collect Data
  3. Explore, understand, and prepare data
  4. Identify the Methodological Approach
  5. Partition Data
  6. Build and Evaluate Models
  7. Deploy Models and Institutionalize
  8. Maintain Models in Production (Retrain, Rollback, Redeploy)

Data Science Process Walkthrough Part 1

ND045 DSBL L01 Data Science Process Walkthrough Part 1 V2

Walkthrough Summary

A digital telehealth startup receives messages of all kinds from its patients.

  • One patient may want a copy of last month's invoice.
  • A second patient might want to know more about switching from a daily formulation to a monthly formulation.
  • A third patient might be reporting side effects such as headaches, dizziness, and arm numbness after starting a new medication.

Clearly these messages differ in the content; the first might be appropriate for customer support, whereas the other two might need to go directly to a medical provider. Moreover, they differ in urgency. The third patient, dealing with critical side effects of a new medication, clearly has a more urgent need than the second patient who is just curious about changing the frequency with which the medication is taken.

As a startup grows, these messages might be handled by a single person processing them in a queue and sending them to either customer support or medical support. As volume grows, however, this soon becomes unwieldy, and an automated process for classifying, and routing these messages might be desired.

Data Science Process Walkthrough Part 2

ND045 DSBL L01 Data Science Process Walkthrough Part 2

Walkthrough Summary Part 2

  1. Identify the opportunity. Here, a machine learning classifier can be employed to bypass a message queue. This can cut down response times and increase customer satisfaction. Furthermore, we reduce the risks associated with delays in responding to urgent medical needs, which gives an increase in the level of medical care.
  2. Obtain the data. In this case, we will need a large sample of patient messages that vary in their content. This initially may involve simply extracting data from a data warehouse, but in production might require ETL pipelines to bring data to the production system. We will ultimately also need to have the correct classifications for those messages (medical, nonmedical; urgent/nonurgent) so we can train a machine-learning algorithm to do this automatically.
  3. Prepare the data. In this case, we will need the message data and all metadata, that is, data besides message content that includes characteristics of the sender, the timing of the message, and so forth. All this data will need to be cleaned and prepared in a structured form for analysis by a machine learning model.
  4. Identify modeling approaches. Here we are looking at supervised learning with a classification task - messages must be classified as medical or nonmedical and as urgent or nonurgent. As such, our modeling approaches would include classification algorithms, such as deep learning approaches, or iterative approaches such as a random forest or gradient boosted machine. We would also identify approaches for carrying out the natural language processing required of classifying text content. In this case, we may choose, for example, a TF-IDF approach to scoring text features extracted from messages.
  5. Partition our data. We typically choose the majority of data as a training set -- that is, the data that we will use to train the model to classify messages. A common tactic here is to choose, say, 70% of the data as our training data. The remainder of this data represents our test set. This data is held out from model training to provide an unbiased set of data on which to test model performance. We typically randomly partition data to the training and test sets and may stratify this randomization if there are particular characteristics that need to be represented across both datasets.
  6. Build and evaluate models based on our training data. As mentioned before, we may consider deep learning models and other iterative classifiers, such as tree-based methods or a support vector machine. We evaluate based on how accurately they classify messages, according to various fit statistics and model performance measures. We use business context to prioritize the measures that are most critical to evaluate the task at hand. For example, we may prioritize the minimization of false positives or false negatives, or maximize specificity or sensitivity, or optimize for precision or recall. You need not know the definitions of all these measures; they are all characteristics of model fit, and you may pay particular attention to one or another depending on the consequences of the model failing to identify certain priority cases.
  7. Deploy that model. The particulars of deployment will depend on the use case. In this case, we may choose to host the model in a cloud environment, accessible via API calls. Models can also be integrated into applications or can stand alone and generate classifications in more of a batch mode.
  8. Maintain the production model. We must monitor the performance of our model to protect against drift or degraded performance over time. We must periodically retrain our model using a newly updated training set to ensure that we are reflecting the full set of messages that may need to be classified. Finally, we may choose to roll back and redeploy a new model that performs better than the one currently in production.

Data Science Process Notes

Key Terms:

  • KPI: Key Performance Indicator used to measure success.
  • ETL: Extract, Transfer, Load is a process for creating a reliable data pipeline.
  • Training Data: A partition of data used to train your machine learning models.
  • Testing Data: A partition of data used to evaluate your machine learning model.
