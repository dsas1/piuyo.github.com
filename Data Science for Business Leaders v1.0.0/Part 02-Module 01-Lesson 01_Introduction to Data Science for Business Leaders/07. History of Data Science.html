History of Data Science Summary

Some key milestones in the history of Data Science and AI:

  • During World War II, Alan Turing, a British computer scientist, deciphered the German “Enigma” code.
  • In 1940, science fiction writer Isaac Asimov published the first of a series of short stories that would eventually be collected in the 1950 book I, Robot, which postulated intelligent robots and the three rules of robotics.
  • In 1950, Turing set the bar for general artificial intelligence by proposing a computer that would be indistinguishable from a human player. This has become known as the Turing Test.
  • A machine called Ferranti Mark 1 mastered checkers in 1951.
  • In a paper for the 1956 Dartmouth Conference (the first artificial intelligence conference), John McCarthy created the term “Artificial Intelligence”. He also created the LISP programming language which for decades was the lingua franca of machine learning.
    *In 1956, AI researchers such as Marvin Minsky (who founded the AI Laboratory at MIT) focused on a “top-down” approach by programming computers with human behavioral rules.
  • In 1968, the movie 2001: A Space Odyssey featured HAL 9000, an intelligent computer. Marvin Minsky served as an advisor for the movie.
  • Shakey the Robot, which was the first general-purpose mobile robot, made its appearance in 1969. This robot could make decisions based on its perceptions of the surroundings.
  • In 1973, a period known as the “AI Winter” resulted from huge drops in funding because AI initiatives to date hadn’t lived up to expectations.
  • Research into narrow AI, also known as expert systems, started in 1981. These systems focused on providing solutions to specific tasks. Digital Equipment Corporation was among the companies that were successful at narrow AI systems. Successes such as these helped bring the end of the AI Winter.
  • In 1990, Rodney Brooks focused on a “bottom-up” approach to AI by laying the groundwork for the development of neural networks.
  • Later in the 1990s, the Japanese government supported an initiative to develop what they called a fifth-generation computer which used parallel computing to use concurrent logic programming for AI.
  • The Supercomputer Deep Blue (created by IBM) beat chess Grand Master Garry Kasparov in 1997.
  • The bursting of the dotcom bubble in the early 2000s brought a retreat in funding for AI.
  • In 2002, Roomba created the iRobot, an autonomous vacuum cleaner that learned the layout of the owner’s home.
  • The US military began investing in autonomous robots in 2004 and 2005.
  • In 2008, Google released a speech recognition application on the iPhone.
  • At the Shanghai 2010 World Expo, 20 NAO robots danced together.
  • IBM’s Watson emerged victorious over two of the world’s all-time best Jeopardy players in 2011.
  • In 2013, the idea of a robotic exoskeleton for soldiers – known as the Tactical Light Operator Suit (TALOS) – was proposed by the US Special Operations Command. Challenges such as dealing with the weight of power sources sufficient for the suit led the project to be shut down in 2019.
  • In 2014, the Eugene Goostman chatbot is reported to have passed the Turing Test.
  • In 2017, Google’s DeepMind AlphaGo defeated the world’s best Go champion.
  • In 2019, Google’s Alphastar defeated professional Starcraft II players with a score of 10-0.
